AMD has just rewritten the rulebook for graphics card performance by smashing the GPU frequency world record with their upcoming Radeon RX 9060 XT, hitting an astonishing 4.769 GHz. This milestone isn't just impressive on paper—it's a clear signal that AMD's RDNA 4 architecture is ready to take the fight to NVIDIA's next-gen offerings, and bargain hunters should be paying close attention.
The record was set by renowned overclocker "1usmus" using a custom liquid nitrogen (LN2) cooling setup, pushing a pre-production sample of the RX 9060 XT well beyond its expected boost clocks. For context, most high-end GPUs like the current Radeon X or GeForce RTX 4090 typically boost to around 2.8-3.0 GHz under normal air or AIO cooling conditions. This achievement suggests AMD has significantly refined the power delivery and thermal management of its RDNA 4 silicon, with substantial headroom built into the architecture. This headroom could directly translate to better sustained clocks and performance-per-pound in retail models, especially when paired with robust third-party cooler designs from partners like Sapphire, PowerColor, and ASRock.
## What's the story?
The Radeon RX 9060 XT isn't just another mid-range GPU—it's shaping up to be a serious contender in the crucial £400-600 segment that represents the sweet spot for most PC gamers. AMD's RDNA 4 architecture is expected to deliver substantial improvements across the board: a redesigned compute unit layout for better rasterisation, next-generation ray accelerators for a major leap in ray tracing performance (a traditional Radeon weakness), and dedicated AI acceleration hardware for features like upscaling and eration.
The 4.769 GHz record demonstrates that AMD has addressed the thermal density and power delivery challenges that previously limited clock speeds on their GPUs. Early whispers from board partners suggest RDNA 4 chips may utilise a more advanced chiplet design, similar to Ryzen CPUs, separating the core graphics complexes from the memory cache and I/O dies. This could improve yields and allow for higher clocks on the GCD (Graphics Compute Die). Furthermore, the move to faster 20 Gbps or even 24 Gbps GDDR7 memory, already confirmed for the high-end RDNA 4 models, will provide the necessary bandwidth to feed a GPU running at such extreme clock speeds, especially at 1440p and 4K.
What makes this particularly interesting is the strategic timing. With NVIDIA's RTX 50 series (Blackwell) reportedly facing production delays and initial supply constraints into late 2025 or even early 2026, AMD appears poised to capture significant market share with a competitively priced, high-performance alternative. The RX 9060 XT is widely expected to launch with a UK SRP between £449 and £499, positioning it directly against the current [RTX 4070 Super](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=NVIDIA+RTX+4070+Super+graphics+card&tag=ctrlaltstock-21) (which sits at £550-£600) and potentially undercutting the anticipated RTX 5070 when it arrives.
The overclocking community is already buzzing about the implications. Members of our CtrlAltStock Discord have been analysing the 1usmus run, which was validated with a 3DMark Time Spy Extreme score that would place it ahead of an RTX 4090 in that specific benchmark—a testament to pure clock speed. The consensus among our members? We could be looking at a GPU that punches well above its weight class in traditional raster games, while its ray tracing and AI performance, while improved, will need to be validated against NVIDIA's mature DLSS 3/4 ecosystem.

## What does this mean for prices?
AMD's frequency record is a powerful psychological and engineering signal that could trigger an aggressive price war in the mid-to-high-end [GPU](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-adrenalin-26-2-2-rx-7000-stability-update) market. Here's what we're watching, with concrete UK pricing:
**Current Competitive Landscape (Retail Prices, GBP):**
- **[NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070 Super](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=NVIDIA+RTX+4070+Super+graphics+card&tag=ctrlaltstock-21):** £550 - £620 (Founders Edition and AIB models)
- **[AMD Radeon RX 7800 XT](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=AMD+RX+7800+XT+graphics+card&tag=ctrlaltstock-21):** £480 - £540 (often on sale below £500)
- **NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4070 Ti Super:** £680 - £770
- **AMD Radeon RX 7900 GRE:** £550 - £600 (a powerful wildcard in this range)
With the RX 9060 XT targeting £449-£499, we expect immediate and aggressive pricing responses. History, particularly from the RX 6000 vs RTX 30 series era, suggests that when AMD launches a truly competitive product at an aggressive price point, NVIDIA responds with price cuts and enhanced bundle offers (like [free games](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/prime-backgammon-free-grab-classic-board-game) or higher-tier subscriptions) on their existing lineup to clear inventory ahead of their next generation.
This could mean the RTX 4070 Super dropping firmly into the £500-£550 bracket, or even seeing temporary sub-£500 deals during key sales periods like Black Friday or Prime Day. The [RX 7800 XT](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=AMD+RX+7800+XT+graphics+card&tag=ctrlaltstock-21), already a fantastic value, may see further discounts to clear stock ahead of the 9060 XT's arrival, potentially dipping towards £450.
The frequency record also hints at better binning of silicon out of the factory. If RDNA 4 chips are inherently capable of higher clocks at lower voltages, more retail units will be able to sustain higher boost frequencies without requiring exotic cooling. This should improve effective yield rates and potentially lower per-unit production costs, giving AMD more flexibility on pricing and board partners more margin to create compelling custom models.
