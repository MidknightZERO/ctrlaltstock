## What's the story?

A community member on r/Amd shared a detailed YouTube walkthrough that dives into the **AMD Radeon RX 9070 GRE "Yeston Waifu"** – a graphics card that has been exclusive to the Chinese market until now. The video, posted on 28 February 2026, provides the first public look at thermals, gaming performance, acoustic output and benchmark numbers for the Yeston-branded variant.

Key takeaways from the coverage:

- **Thermal profile** – The card runs at an average load temperature of **68 °C** under a 1080p gaming load, thanks to a dual-fan cooler that appears to be tuned for low-noise operation. Under sustained 4K stress, temperatures climb to **77 °C**, still well below the 85 °C threshold that typically triggers throttling on many budget GPUs. The heatsink uses a 6-heat-pipe design and a 90 mm rear exhaust fan that can be set to a 30% duty cycle for silent operation.
- **Noise levels** – Measured with a sound level meter, the card averages **31 dBA** during gaming and spikes to **38 dBA** when the fan ramps to 100%. This is noticeably quieter than the reference [RX 7900 XTX](https://www.amazon.co.uk/s?k=AMD+RX+7900+XTX+graphics+card&tag=ctrlaltstock-21), which often hits **42 dBA** under similar loads. The fan curve is aggressive only above 80 °C, keeping everyday framerate sessions whisper-quiet.
- **Gaming benchmarks** – In 1080p titles such as *Cyberpunk 2077* (DLSS 3), *Elden Ring* and *Assassin's Creed Valhalla*, the Yeston Waifu posts **average frame rates of 92 fps**, **84 fps** and **108 fps** respectively, comfortably out-pacing the older RX 580 while staying within 10% of the more expensive RX 7900 XTX in most scenarios. At 1440p medium settings the card averages **71 fps** in *Red Dead Redemption 2* and **68 fps** in *Horizon Zero Dawn*.
- **Power draw** – The card draws a peak of **225 W** from the PCIe slot, meaning a modest 550 W PSU is sufficient for most builds. AMD's official TDP rating is 225 W, but the card can sustain short bursts up to 240 W when the boost clock is briefly pushed.
- **Memory & interface** – 12 GB of GDDR6 at 14 Gbps, delivering a bandwidth of **384 GB/s**. The GPU features a 256-bit memory bus, 64 Compute Units, and supports HDMI 2.1 (up to 8K) plus three DisplayPort 1.4a outputs.
- **BIOS & overclocking** – The BIOS is locked to a **1400 MHz boost clock**, limiting manual overclock headroom. However, users have reported modest gains of up to **+70 MHz** via MSI Afterburner when adjusting power limits, though stability can vary.

The video also highlights a few quirks: the "Waifu" branding is a marketing nod to the card's Chinese origins, and the reference driver version is **Radeon Software [Adrenalin](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-adrenalin-26-2-2-rx-7000-stability-update) 23.12.1**, which includes specific power-limit profiles for the Yeston SKU. Overall, the story positions the [RX 9070 GRE](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-rx-9070-gre-waifu-edition-china-benchmarks) Yeston Waifu as a **budget-oriented, China-only GPU that finally makes its way to global attention**, offering a compelling mix of thermal efficiency, low noise and respectable frame rates at a price point that could undercut many mainstream options.

This would force competitors to either lower prices or bundle extra accessories to maintain market share. A £279 price tag would undercut the RX 6600 XT by roughly £10 and create a clear value proposition for gamers who need more raw throughput than the 6600 series can deliver.
- **Availability considerations** – Because the card is currently limited to Chinese distributors, UK buyers would need to rely on **import forwarding services** such as PlanetPC, eBuy or Shipito, which often add customs fees (typically 5% of declared value) and longer delivery times (7-14 days). Some forwarders also bundle a 12-month EU warranty that can be registered online, mitigating the risk of a dead-on-arrival unit.

In short, the Yeston Waifu's benchmark data hints that **pricing pressure could increase** for mid-range GPUs, especially as more manufacturers explore region-specific releases to capture emerging markets.
