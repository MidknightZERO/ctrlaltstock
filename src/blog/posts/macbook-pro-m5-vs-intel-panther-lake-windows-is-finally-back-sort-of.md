**What does this mean for prices?**

The Reddit user’s experience points towards a few key price implications for UK buyers. Firstly, the demand for high-end Intel processors like the i9-13900K might see fluctuations based on this trend. Currently, the i9-13900K typically retails for around £450-£500 at UK retailers like Overclockers UK or Scan, offering exceptional value for its multi-threaded performance against Apple's top-tier laptop chips. If demand for Windows builds grows, we might see competitive pricing or even discounts, especially during major sales events like Black Friday or Boxing Day. However, the RTX 4090 remains a premium GPU, with UK prices holding firm between £1,600 and £1,900 for models from ASUS, Gigabyte, or MSI, due to its unrivaled performance crown and limited competition at the very top end. Components like DDR5 RAM and fast NVMe SSDs have seen price stabilization, with 32GB kits of DDR5-6000 now commonly available for £100-£130, and 2TB PCIe 4.0 SSDs like the Samsung 990 Pro or WD Black SN850X frequently dipping below £150 on sale. This commoditisation of supporting components further improves the cost-performance ratio of desktop builds over time.

For Apple, the pressure may manifest not in direct price cuts on the MacBook Pro, but in increased value perception for the M-series ecosystem. The M5's performance-per-watt and integrated capabilities will likely remain best-in-class for thin-and-light pro laptops, justifying its premium for mobile professionals. However, for a desktop-bound user who values peak performance and the ability to upgrade a single component (like the GPU) every few years, the Windows/Intel/NVIDIA path now offers a more compelling long-term financial case. The initial outlay is higher, but the amortised cost over the system's life can be lower, and the performance ceiling is vastly higher. The UK market, with its robust PC component retail sector and VAT-inclusive pricing, makes this comparison particularly transparent for consumers willing to do the research.
