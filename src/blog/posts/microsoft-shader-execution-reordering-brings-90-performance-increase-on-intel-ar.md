Imagine getting a massive performance boost for your graphics card, not from new hardware, but from a clever software tweak. That’s precisely what Microsoft's Shader Execution Reordering (SER) technology is promising, and the numbers are staggering: up to 90% performance increases on Intel Arc B-series and 80% on NVIDIA's upcoming Blackwell GPUs. For PC enthusiasts and budget-conscious gamers, this isn't just a technical footnote; it's a potential earthquake for future GPU value and performance, especially in the demanding world of ray tracing. Ray tracing, while visually stunning, has historically been a significant performance hog, often requiring top-tier graphics cards to run smoothly at playable frame rates. A software-driven optimisation of this magnitude could democratise high-fidelity ray-traced experiences, making them accessible to a wider range of hardware and budgets.
## What's the story?
The tech world is buzzing with news of Microsoft's Shader Execution Reordering (SER), a groundbreaking optimisation integrated directly into DirectX. This isn't just a minor improvement; reports indicate truly transformative performance gains for next-generation graphics cards. Specifically, Intel's upcoming Arc B-series GPUs could see an incredible 90% boost, while NVIDIA's highly anticipated Blackwell architecture is projected to achieve an 80% uplift. These figures are not incremental; they represent a fundamental shift in how GPUs process complex lighting and shadow effects, particularly those enabled by ray tracing. This advancement is expected to be a cornerstone feature within future iterations of DirectX, likely building upon the capabilities introduced with DirectX 12 Ultimate, which brought hardware-accelerated ray tracing, variable rate shading, mesh shaders, and sampler feedback to the platform.
At its core, Shader Execution Reordering is about making ray tracing more efficient. Modern GPUs are fantastic at parallel processing, but ray tracing workloads often involve "divergent" shader execution – meaning different parts of the scene require different calculations, which can slow things down as the GPU has to wait for all threads to catch up. Imagine a single ray of light hitting a scene: it might bounce off a metallic surface, then pass through a pane of glass, and finally illuminate a rough concrete wall. Each interaction requires a different type of shader (e.g., metallic reflection, refraction, diffuse lighting). When these different shader types are processed in a random, interleaved order, the GPU’s execution units can become inefficient, switching contexts frequently. SER intelligently reorders these shader workloads, grouping similar calculations together. This allows the GPU to process them more cohesively and efficiently, reducing idle time and dramatically speeding up the rendering process. Think of it like sorting a messy pile of papers into neat stacks before processing them, rather than picking them up randomly. By streamlining the execution flow, SER ensures that the dedicated ray tracing hardware within the GPU, such as NVIDIA's RT Cores or AMD's Ray Accelerators, can operate at maximum efficiency, processing more rays per clock cycle.
This technology is a significant evolution from earlier ray tracing optimisations. While NVIDIA's RTX series introduced hardware acceleration for ray tracing with dedicated RT Cores (first seen in the GeForce RTX 20 series, launched in 2018) and AMD followed suit with their RDNA 2 architecture (found in the Radeon RX 6000 series, released in 2020), SER operates at a deeper software level, optimising the *flow* of data to these dedicated units. Its integration into DirectX means developers can leverage it more easily across a wide range of games and applications, ensuring broad adoption and impact. This isn't a proprietary feature locked to one vendor; it's a platform-level enhancement that aims to benefit the entire ecosystem. This means that not only Intel and NVIDIA but also AMD's future RDNA 4 and subsequent architectures are highly likely to see substantial benefits from SER, making ray tracing performance a more level playing field across the major GPU manufacturers.
For Intel, this news is particularly exciting. Their current Arc A-series GPUs, like the Arc A770 (typically available for around £280-£350) and A750 (often found for £200-£250), have shown promising rasterisation capabilities for their price point, often competing with NVIDIA's GeForce RTX 3060 or AMD's Radeon RX 6600 XT. However, they often lagged behind NVIDIA and AMD in raw ray tracing performance, sometimes struggling to maintain playable frame rates in demanding titles with RT enabled, even at 1080p. With a potential 90% increase on their B-series, Intel could become a much more formidable player in the mid-range and potentially even high-end GPU market, offering compelling ray tracing performance that was previously out of reach. For NVIDIA, the 80% boost for Blackwell means their next-generation cards will push the boundaries of visual fidelity and speed even further, solidifying their position at the pinnacle of GPU performance. This effectively raises the baseline for what we can expect from future graphics cards in terms of advanced rendering techniques, allowing for more complex and realistic lighting simulations without the crippling performance penalties seen today.
