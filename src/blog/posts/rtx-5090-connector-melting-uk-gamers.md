A year after NVIDIA's RTX 5090 launched, reports of 16-pin connector melting continue to surface—raising serious concerns for UK gamers who've invested in the flagship Blackwell GPU. With power demands hitting 575W, these failures aren't just inconvenient; they could damage your entire system.
## What's the story?
The 16-pin connector melting issue isn't new to the RTX 50 series. It first emerged with the [RTX 4090](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-ryzen-500-medusa-rdna-4m-igpu-shows-rdna-4-matrix-isa-support-fsr-4-support-) back in 2022, when users began reporting charred and melted connectors on their £1,600+ graphics cards. Despite NVIDIA and its board partners (AIBs) implementing various fixes over the years, the problem persists—and the RTX 5090 appears to be the latest victim.
Since February 2025, when the first RTX 5090 connector failure was documented, reports have continued weekly. The pattern is consistent: users experience sudden system shutdowns, discover melted plastic around the 12V-2x6 connector (the official name for the 16-pin power connector), and in some cases, find damaged PCIe slots on their motherboards.
What makes this particularly frustrating for UK gamers is the cost involved. At £2,049 for the Founders Edition and often £2,200+ for premium AIB models like the ASUS ROG Astral at £2,399 or the MSI Suprim Liquid X at £2,499, the RTX 5090 represents a significant investment. When connector failures occur, the damage typically extends beyond just the [graphics card](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/graphics-card-review-2026)—potentially destroying hundreds of pounds worth of additional components.
The technical investigation reveals several contributing factors. The 12V-2x6 connector was designed to handle up to 600W, but real-world conditions often push it beyond safe operating temperatures. Poor contact between the connector pins and the PSU cable, combined with inadequate cooling in some PC cases, creates hot spots that eventually lead to melting.
NVIDIA and manufacturers have attempted various mitigation strategies, including improved connector designs, better thermal monitoring, and updated power delivery firmware. However, these measures haven't eliminated the problem entirely. Some users report that even with the latest firmware and certified cables, their RTX 5090 connectors still show signs of heat stress after extended gaming sessions.
The issue has affected specific models disproportionately. The Zotac RTX 5090 Trinity OC has been mentioned in multiple failure reports, while Gigabyte's Aorus Master and Gaming OC models have shown better reliability. This suggests that board design and cooling implementation play crucial roles in preventing failures.

## What does this mean for prices?
The ongoing connector issues have created an interesting dynamic in the UK [GPU](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-adrenalin-26-2-2-rx-7000-stability-update) market. While [RTX 5090](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/mwc-2026-all-the-phones-gadgets-and-announcements-from-barcelona) prices have remained relatively stable—with the Founders Edition hovering around £2,049 and AIB models ranging from £2,199 to £2,499—the resale value has taken a hit.
Second-hand RTX 5090 cards are selling for approximately 15-20% below retail price, as buyers factor in the potential risk of connector failure. This discount reflects the additional cost of preventative measures or potential repairs that buyers might need to undertake.
For those considering purchasing an RTX 5090 in the UK, the current market offers both opportunities and risks. Retailers like Overclockers UK, Scan Computers, and Ebuyer occasionally run promotions, bringing prices down to around £1,999 for Founders Edition models. However, these deals often come with the caveat that buyers should invest in high-quality, certified 16-pin cables and potentially additional cooling solutions.
The connector issues have also boosted demand for alternative high-end GPUs. AMD's RX 7900 XTX, priced around £749, has seen increased interest from gamers who want high performance without the connector concerns. Similarly, last-generation RTX 4090 cards (£1,399-£1,599) are selling well as they represent a safer investment despite slightly lower performance.
Interestingly, the UK's strict consumer protection laws under the Consumer Rights Act 2015 have provided some recourse for affected users. Several UK gamers have successfully claimed refunds or replacements from retailers when their RTX 5090 cards suffered connector failures within the first six months of purchase.

## What to buy right now
If you're in the market for a high-end GPU and want to avoid connector melting risks entirely, here are your best options in the UK market:
**RTX 4090** - At £1,399-£1,599, this card offers 90% of the RTX 5090's performance without the connector concerns. The 4090 has proven reliable over its two-year lifespan, and most units now come with improved 16-pin cables from the factory. The ASUS ROG Strix RTX 4090 OC at £1,549 and the MSI RTX 4090 SUPRIM X at £1,599 are particularly well-regarded.
**RX 7900 XTX** - Priced around £749, this AMD card delivers excellent 4K performance and uses traditional 8-pin power connectors, eliminating any melting concerns. It's particularly good value for 1440p and 4K gaming at high refresh rates. The Sapphire Nitro+ RX 7900 XTX at £799 offers superior cooling and quieter operation.
**RTX 5080** - If you need the latest architecture, the RTX 5080 (£949-£1,099) uses less power and has shown fewer connector issues than its flagship sibling. It still offers excellent performance for 4K gaming and DLSS 4 features. The Gigabyte RTX 5080 Gaming OC at £999 represents good value in this segment.
**RTX 5070 Ti** - For those wanting newer technology at a lower price point, the RTX 5070 Ti (£649-£749) has proven more reliable than the 5090 while still delivering excellent 1440p and respectable 4K performance.
For those who already own an RTX 5090 or are determined to get one, investing in proper accessories is crucial. High-quality 16-pin cables from manufacturers like CableMod (£50-£80) or official NVIDIA cables (£30-£40) can significantly reduce risk. Additionally, ensuring your case has good airflow and monitoring GPU temperatures during long gaming sessions can help prevent issues.
Some UK PC builders have reported success with aftermarket cooling solutions specifically designed for the 12V-2x6 connector area. Products like the CableMod 5090 cooling bracket (£45) and the EKWB 5090 water block (£249) provide additional thermal headroom, though they represent significant additional investment.
