Anker’s Soundcore brand has long been a dominant force in the competitive budget audio market, consistently delivering impressive value with models like the original Soundcore Space. The successor, the Soundcore Space 2, represents a significant evolution rather than a simple iteration. It takes the winning formula of affordability and solid performance and elevates it with a suite of features that directly challenge mid-range offerings. For UK consumers navigating a crowded landscape of true wireless earbuds and over-ear headphones, the Space 2 positions itself as a compelling all-rounder. This [review](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-strix-halo-128-gb-ram-in-a-13-inch-convertible-asus-proart-px13-gopro-editio) dives deep into whether this evolution justifies its modest price bump and how it stands against both its predecessor and the broader market, including its relevance for pairing with next-gen consoles like the PS5.
## Design and Build: Familiar Foundations, Refined Execution
The Soundcore Space 2 retains the overall aesthetic philosophy of its predecessor—clean, minimalist, and functional. The design language is safe and inoffensive, favouring matte plastics and subtle branding over flashy aesthetics. This is a headphone built for practicality, not for making a fashion statement. The headband and ear cups are generously padded with [memory](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/rtx-50-micron-gddr7-memory-supplier-diversification) foam, which provides a comfortable fit for extended listening sessions, a crucial factor for long commutes or work-from-home days. The clamp force is well-calibrated; secure enough to maintain a seal for effective noise cancellation without inducing the "headphone headache" after an hour.
From a build quality perspective, the materials feel durable yet lightweight. The plastic components don’t exhibit the cheap flex sometimes found in the lowest tier of budget headphones. The folding mechanism is robust, allowing the ear cups to swivel flat for easier storage in the included hard case. For a UK user, the portability is a key advantage, fitting neatly into a backpack or suitcase without adding significant bulk. While it doesn't match the premium feel of aluminium-clad rivals, it surpasses expectations for its price point, suggesting Anker’s focus is on internal components and software rather than exterior luxury.

## Sound Quality: A Tunable and Balanced Profile
The heart of any headphone is its sound signature, and here the Space 2 makes noticeable strides. It utilises 40mm dynamic drivers, the same size as the original, but with revised tuning. The default sound profile out of the box is impressively balanced. The bass is present and punchy, providing satisfying impact for electronic, hip-hop, and action movie soundtracks without overwhelming the mid-range. The mids, where vocals and most instruments sit, are clear and well-defined. This clarity is a significant upgrade from the sometimes recessed mids of the first-generation Space, making these headphones far more versatile for podcasts, acoustic music, and spoken word.
The treble is crisp without being sibilant or harsh, a common pitfall for budget drivers. Soundcore has clearly paid attention to tonal balance. Furthermore, the companion Soundcore app (available on iOS and Android) is a game-changer. It includes a customisable 22-band EQ, allowing users to tailor the sound signature to their exact preferences. This level of granular control is typically reserved for much more expensive headphones. For a UK audiophile on a budget, this tunability means the Space 2 can be shaped to sound good with virtually any genre, from the intricate layers of a classical piece to the compressed dynamics of a pop chart-topper. The codec support includes SBC, AAC, and aptX, ensuring reliable, high-quality wireless streaming from both iPhone and Android devices, a crucial consideration for the diverse UK smartphone market.

## Active Noise Cancellation: Catching Up to the Competition
Active Noise Cancellation (ANC) is where the Space 2 makes its most tangible leap forward. The original Space had ANC, but it was largely a novelty at its price. The Space 2’s system is markedly more effective. Using multiple microphones, it does a commendable job of attenuating low-frequency, constant noises—the hum of an airplane engine, the rumble of a train, or the drone of an office air conditioning unit. It won’t silence sudden, sharp noises like a clap or a shout, but for creating a serene listening bubble during travel or in a noisy café, it performs admirably.
The ANC effectiveness is further enhanced by the excellent passive isolation from the plush ear cups. The seal is consistent and effective. The app also offers multiple ANC modes: Transport, Indoor, and Custom. The Transport mode is optimised for consistent low-frequency noise, while Indoor mode targets mid-frequency chatter and ambient office sounds. This contextual awareness is a premium feature that genuinely improves usability. For the UK commuter regularly on the Tube, overground trains, or buses, this adaptive approach means better noise reduction in different environments. A transparency mode is also included, allowing外界 sound to pass through without removing the headphones, useful for airport announcements or quick conversations at a coffee shop.
## Battery Life and Charging: All-Day Endurance
Battery life is another cornerstone of Soundcore’s value proposition, and the Space 2 does not disappoint. Anker claims up to 40 hours of playback with ANC enabled, and real-world usage closely aligns with this figure. At moderate volumes with ANC on, achieving 35-38 hours is realistic. Turning ANC off extends this to a staggering 50+ hours, making these headphones virtually immune to "battery anxiety" on long-haul flights or multi-day work trips.
Charging is via a USB-C port, supporting fast charging. A 5-minute charge reportedly provides around 4 hours of playback, which is exceptionally useful for a last-minute dash out the door. The included cable is of good quality. For the UK user, this means no need for proprietary chargers or carrying multiple cables; a standard USB-C phone charger will suffice. The battery health management in the app is a thoughtful touch, allowing users to set a charging limit (e.g., 80%) to prolong long-term battery health, a feature increasingly important for devices meant to last years.

## Features and Connectivity: The Smart Package
Beyond the core audio and ANC, the Space 2 is packed with thoughtful features. Bluetooth 5.3 ensures a stable, low-latency connection. The multipoint connectivity is particularly useful, allowing simultaneous connection to a laptop and a smartphone. You can be on a Teams call from your PC and seamlessly switch to a phone call without manual re-pairing—a major productivity booster for hybrid workers.
The onboard controls are tactile and intuitive: a single button on each ear cup handles play/pause, volume, track skipping, and calls. The controls are customisable via the app. The microphone quality for calls is surprisingly good for the price, with clear voice pickup and decent noise rejection on the caller’s end. The wear-detection sensor automatically pauses playback when you remove the headphones, resuming when you put them back on. This small feature dramatically improves the user experience.
## Comparison and Value Proposition in the UK Market
How does the Space 2 stack up? Compared to its predecessor, the improvements in sound balance, ANC potency, and app features are substantial enough to warrant the upgrade for most users. Against direct competitors like the [Sony](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/god-of-war-universe-new-franchise) WH-CH720N or the older WH-XB900N, the Space 2 often matches or exceeds them in sound customisation and battery life, while typically undercutting them on price. It doesn’t quite reach the sublime ANC of Sony’s flagship WH-1000XM5, but for roughly half the price, its performance is outstanding.
In the UK retail landscape, the Soundcore Space 2 consistently retails between £120 and £150, depending on sales and colour variants (it often appears in sleek black, white, or blue). This places it in a sweet spot: significantly more capable than sub-£80 models, yet accessible compared to £200+ premium options. For someone wanting a reliable, feature-packed pair of ANC headphones without financial stretch, it’s arguably the best value proposition in its segment. It’s also a fantastic companion for [gaming](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/amd-ryzen-ai-linux-driver-updates-budget-gaming). While not a replacement for a dedicated gaming headset with a microphone, its solid soundstage and effective ANC make it excellent for single-player experiences on a PC or a console
