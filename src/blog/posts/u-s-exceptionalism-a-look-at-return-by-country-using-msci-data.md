## What's the Story? Unpacking Global Economic Performance
A Reddit user on r/StockMarket recently published an in-depth analysis of country-specific stock market returns, drawing on data from MSCI indices going all the way back to the end of 1998. MSCI (Morgan Stanley Capital International) indices are widely recognised benchmarks used by institutional investors to gauge the performance of various equity markets around the world. These indices track hundreds or even thousands of stocks within specific countries or regions, providing a comprehensive snapshot of economic health and investor sentiment. For instance, the MSCI World Index covers large and mid-cap companies across 23 developed markets, while the MSCI Emerging Markets Index focuses on 24 emerging economies.
This comprehensive look across 23 developed countries and 24 emerging markets provides a unique perspective on which economies have been thriving over the past couple of decades. The user meticulously calculated Compound Annual Growth Rates (CAGR) for gross returns, including dividends, all denominated in a constant currency to strip out currency fluctuation effects. CAGR is a particularly useful metric for assessing long-term investment performance as it smooths out volatility and provides an annualised rate of return, making comparisons between different assets or markets over various timeframes much more meaningful. By removing currency fluctuations, the analysis focuses purely on the underlying economic performance of each nation's stock market, rather than the strength or weakness of its currency against, for example, the US Dollar. This is crucial for a true apples-to-apples comparison of economic growth and corporate profitability.
The findings highlight some standout performers across the globe. Among developed nations, Canada led the pack with an impressive +9.92% CAGR, followed closely by Denmark at +9.59% and Norway at +9.41%. Even with recent market volatility, such as a reported crash in Denmark's stock market due to the underperformance of pharmaceutical giant Novo Nordisk, these countries have demonstrated remarkable long-term resilience and growth. Canada's performance is often bolstered by its rich natural resources, particularly in energy and mining, alongside a stable banking sector. Norway benefits significantly from its vast oil and gas reserves, managed through its colossal sovereign wealth fund, which invests globally and provides a stabilising effect. Denmark, despite its relatively small size, has nurtured world-leading companies in pharmaceuticals (like the aforementioned Novo Nordisk, a global leader in diabetes and obesity treatments), renewable energy, and shipping, driving consistent investor returns. The Reddit analysis also pointed to other strong performers among developed markets, including Australia, often benefiting from its commodity exports and strong ties to Asian growth, and Switzerland, known for its stable economy, robust financial sector, and multinational pharmaceutical and luxury goods companies.
The analysis also shed light on the performance of the United States, a market often perceived as the global powerhouse, particularly given the dominance of its tech giants. While the specific CAGR for the US was not explicitly detailed in the initial snippet, the 'U.S. Exceptionalism' headline hints at its strong performance, likely placing it among the top tier of developed markets.
