# Virtual Boy Review:Nintendo's Oddest Switch Accessory Yet Is an Immersive ’90s Museum  

**Hook** – The Nintendo Virtual Boy Adapter (model **VB‑01**) landed on UK shelves today, and it’s already turning heads. For bargain hunters who love a good nostalgia-driven novelty, this quirky piece of kit promises a trip back to the red-monochrome world of the mid-’90s – all while slotting neatly into a modern Switch dock. If you’ve ever wondered what it feels like to stare through a pair of retro VR goggles without breaking the bank, now’s the time to find out.

## What’s the story?  

### A fresh museum piece for a modern console  

Nintendo surprised the gaming community on 20 February 2026 with the **Virtual Boy Adapter (VB‑01)**, billed as a way to experience a slice of gaming history. This small, plastic accessory plugs into a Switch dock, connecting via USB-C and providing a 3.5mm audio jack for headphones. Its core function is to transform the Switch into a modern, albeit limited, replica of the original Virtual Boy console. The VB‑01 isn’t a full Virtual Boy revival but a bridge, allowing Switch owners to play a curated selection of Virtual Boy games using the original hardware’s distinctive, head-mounted display (HMD) design.

## How does it work?  

### Plugging into the past  

Setting up the VB‑01 is straightforward but requires a few steps. First, plug the USB-C cable into your Switch dock. Then, connect the 3.5mm audio cable from the VB‑01 to your headphones or speakers. The VB‑01 itself connects to the Switch dock via a dedicated port. Crucially, you’ll also need the included USB-C to USB-A power adapter (a separate purchase for £9.99 on the Nintendo UK website) to power the HMD unit. Once powered, the VB‑01’s red-tinted goggles (model **VR‑G1**) are worn like VR headsets of today, displaying games in a single, fixed red monochrome display. The Switch screen remains off, creating a unique, isolated gaming experience.

## The experience  

### A monochrome museum piece  

Playing with the VB‑01 is a trip. The red monochrome visuals, reminiscent of the original Virtual Boy’s 1995 launch, create a stark, immersive environment. Games like **Mario’s Tennis** (1995) and **Red Alarm** (1995) showcase the limitations of the original hardware – blocky graphics, no color, and a distinct 3D illusion achieved through parallax scrolling. The experience feels more like a museum exhibit than a practical gaming device today. The "3D" effect is subtle, relying on the brain’s perception of depth from the red/green separation, but it’s not true stereoscopic 3D. The lack of modern features like motion controls or online play is immediately apparent, highlighting why the Virtual Boy was discontinued after just 13 months in 1995.

## The pros and cons  

### Nostalgia vs. practicality  

**Pros:**  
*   **Authentic nostalgia:** For £59.99 (the MSRP for the VB‑01 unit alone), you get a tangible piece of gaming history. It’s a conversation starter and a unique collectible.  
*   **Portability:** The HMD is lightweight and easy to store.  
*   **Curated library:** Nintendo offers a small but authentic selection of Virtual Boy classics.  
*   **Unique experience:** Playing in red monochrome offers a distinct, albeit niche, atmosphere.  

**Cons:**  
*   **Monochrome limitation:** The red-only display is visually jarring and lacks the original’s promised color.  
*   **No modern features:** No motion controls, online play, or enhanced graphics.  
*   **Hardware limitations:** The original Virtual Boy’s games were notoriously difficult and lacked depth.  
*   **Price vs. value:** At £59.99 for the adapter and £9.99 for the power brick, the total cost (£69.98) is steep for a limited experience.  
*   **Comfort:** Extended play can cause eye strain due to the fixed focus and red tint.

## The Virtual Boy’s legacy  

### Why it was discontinued  

The original Virtual Boy (released in 1995) was a commercial and critical failure. Its monochrome display, high price (£179 in 1995, equivalent to roughly £360 today), and uncomfortable design led to poor sales. Nintendo discontinued it after just 13 months. The VB‑01’s existence is a testament to the enduring fascination with this infamous console. It allows modern gamers to experience a small part of its history without needing the original, bulky hardware.

## Final verdict  

### A museum piece, not a must-buy  

The Nintendo Virtual Boy Adapter (VB‑01) is less a practical gaming accessory and more a meticulously crafted museum piece for Switch owners. It delivers a faithful, albeit limited, recreation of the original Virtual Boy’s core experience – red monochrome visuals and a sense of retro immersion. While the £69.98 price tag (adapter + power brick) is steep for a niche experience, it’s a unique way to connect with gaming history. If you’re a hardcore Nintendo collector or a deep-dive retro gaming enthusiast, it’s a worthwhile curiosity. For everyone else, the original Virtual Boy’s legacy remains best appreciated through documentaries and its preserved hardware in museums, not through this modern re-interpretation.
