If you’re hunting for a [bargain](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/micron-gddr7-rtx-50-gpu-deals) on the Xbox Series X this year, you might be surprised how much the console’s dashboard visual style can influence your perception of value. A recent thread on r/XboxSeriesX asked members simply: *“What do you think about the visual style (not layout) of the current Xbox Dashboard?”* The answer sparked a lively debate that highlights how Microsoft’s design language has evolved – and why it matters when you’re deciding whether to snap up a console now or wait for a rumoured refresh later in the year.

## What’s the story?  

One Reddit user summed up their feelings in a concise post, attaching a screenshot of the dashboard for reference:  

> “I personally like the design. It’s elegant and minimal without being **too** minimal, you know. I like the dynamic backgrounds and love the fact that I can choose the colours. I also like the ‘neon’ lines around what is selected. The sounds are cool as well, but I prefer the ones from the early [Xbox](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/billions-of-dollars-later-and-still-nobody-knows-what-an-xbox-is) One Dashboard.” – *u/TechieGamerUK*  

The screenshot (taken from the official Xbox dashboard, available at the original Reddit post) shows a sleek, fluid interface where the Xbox logo glows softly, the background shifts subtly between gradients, and highlighted items are f[ram](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/nvidia-rolls-back-its-latest-driver-update-game-ready-driver-595-59-reportedly-c)ed by thin neon‑blue outlines. Microsoft’s UI team has been rolling out **Fluent Design** elements across the entire Xbox ecosystem, and the Series X dashboard is the most recent incarnation of that philosophy. Fluent Design, introduced with Windows 10, emphasizes motion, depth, and responsiveness, with key principles including **light, shadow, material, and scale**. The dashboard’s dynamic backgrounds, for instance, use parallax scrolling techniques to create a sense of depth, while the neon outlines leverage high-contrast RGB values to stand out against darker themes.  

Another user, *u/GamerInTheNorth*, added: “The dashboard feels like a premium product. Compared to the cluttered Xbox 360 UI, this is a massive step forward. The ability to toggle between light and dark modes without [restart](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/kingdom-come-deliverance-royal-edition-physical-release-is-up-for-pre-order-rest)ing the console is a game-changer.” Meanwhile, a third commenter, *u/ConsoleCollector99*, noted: “I miss the old ‘Xbox’ font and the way the dashboard felt like a gaming hub rather than a generic app. The current design is too sterile for my taste.”  

The dashboard’s visual language is deeply tied to Microsoft’s broader strategy. By adopting Fluent Design, the company aligns the Xbox experience with its Windows and Microsoft 365 apps, ensuring consistency across devices. This is particularly relevant for users who switch between a console and a PC, as the Xbox Dashboard now mirrors the sleek, adaptive interfaces seen in the Xbox App on Windows 11.  

---

### Key visual elements that fans are praising  

- **Dynamic backgrounds** – The dashboard now cycles through a library of colour‑rich, animated scenes that change based on the time of day or user‑selected themes. For example, the “Nature” theme features a simulated sunrise over a mountain range, while the “Retro” theme cycles through 8-bit inspired pixel art. These backgrounds are not just aesthetic; they’re designed to reduce eye strain during late-night gaming sessions by adjusting brightness levels automatically. Users can also upload their own images via the Xbox app, though this feature is still in beta testing.  
- **Customisable colour palettes** – Users can pick from a handful of pre‑set palettes or create their own, letting the console reflect personal taste while still retaining a clean aesthetic. The current options include “Ocean Blue,” “Forest Green,” and “Space Black,” with hex codes like `#003366` for deep blues and `#2E8B57` for vibrant greens. Some users have reported that certain palettes perform better on specific TVs – for instance, the “Space Black” theme has been noted to look sharper on [OLED](https://ctrlaltstock.com/blog/vrr-flickering-with-nvidia-frame-gen-2026) displays.  
- **Neon selection outlines** – A thin, glowing line appears around any item the cursor hovers over, echoing the “neon” aesthetic that many associate with modern UI design. It provides a subtle visual cue that improves discoverability. The neon effect uses a soft glow rather than harsh lighting, which some users argue is more accessible for those with sensitivity to bright colors. The RGB values for the neon outline are set to `(0, 128, 255)` by default, but this can be adjusted in advanced settings.  
- **Sound design** – While the new UI uses a lighter, ambient chime, many veterans still favour the richer, more resonant tones that accompanied the original Xbox One dashboard. The Reddit thread shows a split in opinion, with some users arguing that the older sounds feel more “gaming‑centric.” For example, the new dashboard’s confirmation sound is a subtle *ding*, whereas the original Xbox One used a deeper *whoosh*. Microsoft has not yet announced plans to reintroduce the classic sound pack, though the community’s feedback suggests it could be a low-effort update.
